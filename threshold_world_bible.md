# THRESHOLD

### A World Bible for a Strand Fiction Universe

---

## Part I: The Shape of Things

The sky above Meridian was the color of a dead channel.

Not the romantic nothing of old science fiction — not static, not snow. A dead channel now was a flat commercial blue, the Directorate's proprietary shade, broadcast from atmospheric emitters embedded in the thermosphere like a second ozone layer. The color meant: *we are here. We are above you. We are the sky itself.*

Sera Voss didn't look up. Nobody on Sublevel Eight looked up. The sublevels had their own sky — a pressed concrete ceiling at four meters, yellowed by decades of recycled air and the particular brand of fluorescent tube the Directorate had stopped manufacturing eleven years ago but never replaced. Down here, the light had a frequency that made your teeth itch.

She was running late. The sequencer in her bag was warm against her hip, wrapped in a static sheath that was supposed to fool the biometric sweeps but probably didn't. The sweeps were getting better. Three months ago you could walk through a checkpoint with a pocketed primer cartridge and the scanners would read it as a nicotine inhaler. Now they flagged anything with a Hoogsteen-binding signature above 0.3 nanomolar affinity. The Directorate's detection protocols were evolving faster than the resistance's countermeasures, which was the kind of arms race that only ended one way unless someone changed the terms.

Sera was trying to change the terms.

She turned left at the water reclamation junction, passed through a curtain of thermal baffling that smelled like copper and old milk, and descended a staircase that wasn't on any municipal schematic. Sublevel Nine. Then Ten. Then a door that had been a maintenance access for the geothermal grid before the grid was rerouted, and was now the entrance to a clinic that didn't exist.

The patient was already on the table. Male, mid-thirties, subcutaneous fat distribution suggesting desk work, probably administrative. His shirt was folded neatly on a shelf. The neatness told her more than his intake form — this was a man who organized his fear into right angles.

"You're the sequencer," he said. Not a question.

"I'm the person who's going to thread you." Sera set her bag on the counter, began extracting components. The sequencer itself was a matte-gray cylinder the size of a forearm, unremarkable except for the fact that possessing one carried a mandatory fifteen-year sentence in a Directorate Corrections Facility. "Tell me why."

"Does it matter?"

"It matters to your uptake. Stress hormones interfere with primer integration. The more cortisol in your system, the noisier your expression pathways. So I need you calm, and the fastest way to get you calm is to let you talk."

He talked.

## Part II: Before the Primer

### Year Zero Minus Forty: The Felsenfeld Proof

It began, as most revolutions do, with a quiet observation in a laboratory that no one outside the field noticed.

In 1957, three researchers at the National Institute of Mental Health demonstrated that a third strand of nucleic acid could bind to a DNA double helix. The mechanism was a different kind of hydrogen bonding — not the Watson-Crick pairing that held the two strands together, but something called Hoogsteen pairing, where the purine base rotated to expose a second bonding face. The third strand nestled into the major groove of the double helix like a key into a lock that no one knew existed.

For decades, this was a curiosity. A footnote. Triple-stranded DNA — triplex DNA, H-DNA — was studied in the careful, incremental way that molecular biology studies anything: slowly, with grant applications. Researchers mapped the conditions under which triplexes formed: acidic pH for the pyrimidine motif, neutral pH for the purine motif, divalent cations to neutralize backbone charge repulsion, negative supercoiling to widen the major groove. They discovered that triplex-forming sequences were absurdly common in the human genome — nearly every annotated gene had at least one target site in its promoter or coding region.

They discovered that triplexes could silence genes. A third strand bound at a promoter physically blocked the transcription machinery. One bound in a coding region stalled RNA polymerase at the junction between double and triple helix — the enzyme couldn't unwind both bond types simultaneously. This meant that a precisely designed oligonucleotide could reach into a living cell and shut down a specific gene with a specificity that restriction enzymes and early gene therapy could only dream of.

They discovered that triplexes could edit genes. Conjugated to photoreactive agents like psoralen, a triplex-forming oligonucleotide created interstrand cross-links at exact genomic coordinates. The cell's own repair machinery — nucleotide excision repair, homologous recombination — recognized the distortion and attempted to fix it, but the repair template could be supplied externally, hijacking the process to write new sequence into the genome.

And they discovered — this was the part that would eventually burn the world down — that triplex formation was linked to epigenetic rewriting. The helical distortion caused by a third strand triggered histone deacetylation, which triggered histone methylation, which recruited heterochromatin proteins, which condensed the surrounding DNA into silence. The triplex didn't just block a gene. It locked it. And the lock was heritable.

The inverse was also true. If you could introduce the right triplex at the right site, you could unlock genes that had been epigenetically silenced for generations. Dormant capabilities written into the genome millions of years ago, switched off by evolutionary pressure or developmental programming, could theoretically be reactivated.

Nobody used the word "superpower." The papers said "latent phenotypic expression." The grants said "therapeutic reactivation of silenced loci." But the people who understood what they were reading — and there were very few of them — understood that the implications were not therapeutic.

They were civilizational.

### Year Zero Minus Twelve: The Nakamura Synthesis

Dr. Yuki Nakamura did not set out to change the species. She set out to cure Friedreich's ataxia.

The disease was caused by a trinucleotide repeat expansion — GAA repeats in intron 1 of the FXN gene, sometimes hundreds or thousands of copies where a healthy genome had fewer than forty. The repeats folded back on themselves to form intramolecular triplex structures — H-DNA — which silenced the gene encoding frataxin, a mitochondrial protein essential for iron-sulfur cluster assembly. Without frataxin, mitochondria failed. Neurons died. Patients lost coordination, then mobility, then cardiac function. There was no cure.

Nakamura's approach used peptide nucleic acids — synthetic molecules with a protein-like backbone carrying DNA bases, electrically neutral, invisible to the body's nucleases and proteases. A stealth molecule. Her team designed gamma-modified PNA clamps that bound the GAA repeat region with femtomolar affinity — thousands of times stronger than natural DNA binding — and disrupted the pathological triplex, reopening the chromatin and restoring frataxin expression.

It worked. In mice, then in cell cultures from human patients, then in a Phase I trial that produced the first measurable frataxin recovery in living FRDA patients. The results were published in *Nature Medicine* to the kind of restrained scientific enthusiasm that, in retrospect, was the last quiet moment before the storm.

Because Nakamura's PNA clamps didn't just fix Friedreich's ataxia. They demonstrated, with clinical-grade evidence, that a synthetic molecule could reach into the human genome, disrupt an endogenous triplex structure, reverse an epigenetic silencing cascade, and reactivate a gene that the body had locked shut.

The question was immediate and obvious: what else was locked?

### Year Zero Minus Seven: The Kowloon Paper

The paper that ended the old world was published pseudonymously on an unmoderated preprint server by a research group identifying itself only as "Kowloon." It was titled "Systematic Identification of Epigenetically Suppressed Phenotypic Loci in the Human Genome and Targeted Reactivation via Triplex-Mediated Chromatin Remodeling."

The paper identified 1,247 gene clusters across the human genome that showed evidence of deep epigenetic silencing — methylation patterns and histone modifications consistent with deliberate or evolutionary suppression spanning tens of thousands of years. Many were in regions previously dismissed as junk DNA. The Kowloon group had designed TFO sequences for 340 of these clusters and tested reactivation in human cell lines.

Eighty-nine produced measurable phenotypic changes.

Enhanced mitochondrial output. Accelerated neural myelination. Novel protein synthesis pathways with no known analog in existing biology. Muscle fiber restructuring. Sensory receptor proliferation. In one case — Cluster 77, later designated the "Kowloon Prime" locus — reactivation produced cells that generated measurable electromagnetic fields through what appeared to be a biological analog of magnetoreception, scaled up by several orders of magnitude.

The paper's conclusion, written with the flat affect of people who understood exactly what they had done, was three sentences long: "The human genome contains latent capabilities that were epigenetically suppressed at an indeterminate point in evolutionary history. These capabilities can be reactivated through targeted triplex-mediated chromatin remodeling using synthetic PNA oligonucleotides. The implications for human phenotypic expression are significant."

The preprint was downloaded 2.3 million times in forty-eight hours. Within a week, three independent labs had partially replicated the results. Within a month, the first underground threading clinic opened in Shenzhen.

Within a year, the Directorate existed.

### Year Zero: The Threshold Act

Governments did not act slowly. This is a misconception that survives only because people prefer narratives of institutional incompetence to the more disturbing truth: that institutions can move with breathtaking speed when sufficiently frightened.

The Threshold Act was drafted, ratified, and enacted by the twelve largest economies in eleven weeks. Its provisions were comprehensive: the synthesis, possession, or distribution of triplex-forming oligonucleotides or peptide nucleic acids designed for human genomic modification was classified as a Category One offense. All existing research into targeted epigenetic reactivation was classified and transferred to state control. The Kowloon paper was formally retracted by every hosting server, though it continued to circulate on decentralized networks.

A new regulatory body was established: the Directorate for Genomic Integrity, later shortened by usage to the Directorate. Its mandate was the monitoring, regulation, and where necessary suppression of unauthorized genomic modification. It was given broad surveillance powers, including the deployment of atmospheric biometric scanning arrays, mandatory helix-signature registration for all citizens, and the authority to detain and process individuals whose genomic profiles showed evidence of triplex-mediated modification.

The Directorate's public position was reasonable, measured, and — this was the part that made it so effective — largely correct. Uncontrolled triplex formation caused genomic instability. H-DNA structures could trigger trinucleotide repeat expansion, chromosomal translocation, and mutagenesis. The Kowloon clusters had been epigenetically silenced for a reason, and reactivating them without understanding that reason was playing Russian roulette with the human genome. Early threading attempts produced a non-trivial rate of what the medical literature called "expression cascading" and what the clinics called "burning out" — uncontrolled activation of multiple gene clusters simultaneously, producing catastrophic cellular stress, organ failure, and death.

The Directorate was not wrong about any of this.

What the Directorate did not say — what it went to considerable lengths to prevent anyone from saying — was that controlled, sequential reactivation of specific Kowloon clusters, using properly designed PNA primers with verified binding specificity, was safe. That the technology existed to thread a human being without burning them out. That the risk was not inherent to threading itself but to the absence of precision, training, and quality control — all of which the Directorate possessed and none of which it intended to share.

The Directorate threaded its own people. Of course it did. Its enforcement division, the Helix Integrity Bureau, was staffed by augmented operatives whose expression profiles would have gotten any civilian a mandatory sentence. The official position was that these were "controlled research subjects operating under medical supervision." The unofficial reality was that the Directorate had built the most powerful military and surveillance force in human history by monopolizing the technology it had criminalized.

This is the shape of the world: those who control the third strand control everything. Those who lack it are Baseline — the unaugmented majority, the two-helix population, the citizens whose civic status is "Standard" and whose access to threading is exactly zero. Those who have been threaded outside Directorate authorization are Modified Nationals — civic status "Conditional," which means their housing, employment, medical coverage, and legal protections can be suspended at any time by any Directorate official for any reason.

And those who thread others — the sequencers, the underground clinicians, the people who carry PNA primers in static sheaths through checkpoints designed to find them — are the most wanted individuals on the planet.

## Part III: The World As It Stands

### The Directorate

The Directorate is not a government. It does not replace governments. It exists alongside them, above them, and through them — a parallel authority structure with jurisdiction over anything touching genomic modification, which, in practice, is jurisdiction over everything. It has its own courts (the Genomic Integrity Tribunals), its own enforcement (the Helix Integrity Bureau, or HIB), its own intelligence apparatus (the Office of Strand Monitoring, or OSM), and its own research division (Applied Genomic Sciences, or AGS — the only entity legally permitted to conduct human threading research).

Its power is structural, not theatrical. The Directorate does not need secret police or midnight raids, though it has both. It controls the scanning infrastructure — the atmospheric biometric arrays that detect helix signatures at distance, the checkpoint readers that flag Modified Nationals, the medical databases that track every citizen's genomic baseline. If you are threaded without authorization, you become visible. Every transit checkpoint, every hospital intake, every employment verification reveals you. The Directorate doesn't need to hunt you. It just needs to wait.

The Directorate's internal culture is not monolithic. Three factions compete for influence:

**The Stabilists** believe threading should remain entirely state-controlled, that the technology is inherently dangerous, and that the current system — however imperfect — prevents the catastrophic genomic destabilization that would result from widespread unregulated augmentation. They point to the early threading casualties, to the expression cascading deaths, to the mutagenic risks of H-DNA formation. They are cautious, institutional, and sincere. Many Stabilists genuinely believe they are protecting the species.

**The Ascendants** believe the Directorate should accelerate its own threading programs, creating an augmented ruling class that can manage the complexities of a post-Kowloon civilization. They are comfortable with the hierarchy implied by this — some humans will be enhanced, most will not, and the enhanced will govern. The Ascendants view Baselines not with contempt but with the careful paternalism of people who believe they know better. They are the architects of the Directorate's most ambitious internal programs: Project Crucible (elite operative augmentation), Project Loom (second-generation threading in utero), and the rumored Project Weave (networked consciousness experiments using Kowloon Cluster 77's electromagnetic properties).

**The Indexers** are pragmatists who believe the Directorate should control threading not by banning it but by licensing it. A regulated market. Approved clinics. Standardized primer sequences. Tax revenue. They argue that prohibition has failed — the underground threading rate grows every year — and that a licensing system would let the Directorate maintain its monopoly on the technology while appearing to liberalize access. The Indexers are gaining influence, and their proposals are the most politically dangerous because they are the most reasonable.

These factions matter to the game because their conflicts create event opportunities. A Stabilist purge of Ascendant-funded research produces refugees who need hiding. An Indexer policy trial opens licensed clinics that the resistance views as surveillance traps. An Ascendant breakthrough in Project Loom raises the specter of a generation of children threaded before birth — and the question of whether the resistance should try to stop it.

### The Unbound

The resistance calls itself the Unbound — not because its members are free, but because the name is an aspiration. It is a decentralized network of cells, clinics, couriers, and sympathizers united by the belief that threading should not be the Directorate's monopoly. Beyond that foundational principle, agreement fractures.

**The Weavers' Collective** is the scientific arm — the sequencers, the PNA chemists, the people who actually know how to thread safely. They are cautious, methodical, and profoundly anxious about the quality control problem. Every threading death in an underground clinic is a propaganda victory for the Directorate. The Collective maintains strict protocols, trains new sequencers through apprenticeship, and refuses to thread anyone whose genomic profile shows elevated mutagenic risk. They are the resistance's conscience, and they are frequently at odds with its fighters.

**The Null Current** is the militant wing. Its members are overwhelmingly Threaded — many of them early-generation augments whose expression profiles are unstable, whose bodies bear the visible marks of imperfect threading: bioluminescent scarring, asymmetric tissue growth, tremors from neural overexpression. The Null Current conducts direct action against Directorate infrastructure: scanner sabotage, HIB interdiction, data center raids, and — increasingly — targeted assassination of high-ranking Directorate officials. The Collective views the Null Current as reckless. The Null Current views the Collective as complicit in a system that kills people slowly instead of quickly.

**The Open Strand Initiative** occupies the space between. Its members are propagandists, educators, and community organizers who believe the path to liberation is political rather than violent or scientific. They run underground schools teaching citizens about the Kowloon clusters, about the science the Directorate suppresses, about the real risks and real potential of threading. They operate pirate broadcasts, distribute literature, and maintain the decentralized network that keeps the cells connected. They are the most vulnerable faction because their work requires visibility — you cannot organize people without being seen.

**The Remnant Archive** is the smallest and strangest faction: a group of researchers who believe the Kowloon clusters were not evolved capabilities that were epigenetically suppressed by evolutionary pressure, but engineered capabilities that were deliberately silenced. By whom, and when, is the subject of considerable internal debate. The Archive's position — that the third helix was not invented but *rediscovered* — has the uncomfortable property of being unfalsifiable and increasingly well-supported by the genomic evidence. The silencing patterns on the Kowloon clusters are too uniform, too precise, too coordinated across the genome to be the product of drift. Something, at some point in deep history, reached into the human genome and turned off capabilities that should not have been there in the first place. The Archive wants to know what, and why, and whether it might come back.

### The Zones

The game world is a single planet — Earth, though that name is rarely used. The surface is divided into zones that reflect the post-Threshold political geography:

**Meridian** — the Directorate's administrative capital. A vertical megacity built on and into the bones of three older cities, extending thirty sublevels below ground and two kilometers into the atmosphere. The upper levels are clean, surveilled, climate-controlled, and populated almost exclusively by Directorate personnel, licensed professionals, and registered Baselines with Standard civic status. The sublevels are where the rest of the population lives — in decreasing order of infrastructure quality, rule of law, and Directorate presence. Below Sublevel Fifteen, the Directorate's control is nominal. Below Twenty, it is fictional.

**The Strand** — a coastal industrial zone, formerly a major shipping corridor, now the planet's largest concentration of unlicensed manufacturing. Primer synthesis labs, PNA fabrication workshops, and black-market sequencer repair shops operate alongside legitimate salvage operations and automated factories. The Directorate maintains a perimeter but does not seriously attempt to control the interior. The Strand is where you go to get threaded if you have money. It is also where you go to die if you don't.

**Threshold Basin** — a geothermal basin in what was once a national park, now the site of the Directorate's primary research installation and the location of the original Kowloon replication experiments. The basin's unique geological properties — high ambient mineral concentrations, natural electromagnetic anomalies, elevated background radiation — produce environmental effects on threading that AGS has spent years studying and that the resistance has spent years trying to understand independently. Wild-threaded organisms — animals and plants that have undergone spontaneous triplex formation from environmental exposure — populate the basin's interior, creating an ecology that no one fully understands.

**The Pale** — a buffer zone between Directorate-controlled territory and the unregulated regions, named for the historical term for a boundary beyond which law does not apply. The Pale is where most players will spend their early game: a transitional space where Directorate authority is present but thin, where threading is illegal but common, where faction allegiance is fluid, and where the fundamental choice — order or freedom, control or risk, the Directorate's peace or the Unbound's uncertainty — is not abstract but daily.

**The Deep Latitude** — far from Meridian, beyond the Pale, in territories the Directorate considers too remote or too hostile to administer. Scattered settlements, some Baseline, some exclusively Threaded, some mixed. Rumors of second-generation augments — children of Threaded parents who inherited the third strand without primer intervention — circulate here. The Deep Latitude is endgame territory: high risk, high reward, low law.

## Part IV: The Science of Threading (In-Universe)

*The following is adapted from an Unbound educational document distributed on decentralized networks. The Directorate classifies its possession as a Category Two offense.*

### What Is Threading?

Your DNA is a double helix — two strands wound around each other, held together by hydrogen bonds between paired bases. Adenine to thymine. Cytosine to guanine. Watson-Crick pairing. This is the genome you were born with, the one the Directorate registered at your birth, the one their scanners read every time you pass through a checkpoint.

Threading introduces a third strand.

The third strand binds to the major groove of your existing double helix using a different kind of hydrogen bond — Hoogsteen pairing. The binding is sequence-specific: the primer must match the target region precisely, or it won't attach. When it does, it forms a triple helix — a triplex — at that location. The triplex changes the local geometry of your DNA. The major groove widens. The helix rigidifies. The surrounding chromatin structure shifts.

This shift is the mechanism. The triplex disrupts the epigenetic marks — the methylation patterns, the histone modifications — that have been keeping specific gene clusters silent. These clusters, identified in the Kowloon Paper, encode capabilities that the human genome has carried for millennia but never expressed. Threading unlocks them.

What you can do after threading depends on which clusters are activated, in what sequence, and how your individual genome responds. This is where Phlux comes in.

### What Is Phlux?

Every genome has a measurable instability margin — the degree to which its expression patterns can deviate from baseline before producing errors. The Kowloon group called this the "phenotypic flux coefficient." The clinics call it Phlux.

High Phlux means your genome tolerates deviation well. Your expression pathways are noisy but productive — you'll develop more capabilities, more quickly, but with less predictability. You might unlock electromagnetic perception when you were sequenced for enhanced reflexes. You might develop bioluminescent tissue as a side effect of muscle fiber restructuring. High Phlux individuals are volatile, powerful, and difficult to control — which is why the Directorate tests for it and why individuals above a certain Phlux threshold are flagged for permanent monitoring.

Low Phlux means your genome is stable. Your threading will produce exactly what the primer was designed to produce, nothing more. This is safer. It is also less interesting. Low Phlux operatives are the Directorate's preferred agents: predictable, reliable, and limited.

The word entered the vernacular because someone on a pirate broadcast mispronounced "phenotypic flux" and the corruption stuck. The Directorate uses the clinical term. Everyone else says Phlux.

### What Are Expressions?

When a Kowloon cluster is activated by threading, the new genes begin producing proteins, enzymes, and structural modifications that manifest as observable capabilities. These are called Expressions — the phenotypic output of the third strand.

Expressions are not magic. They are biology operating outside the parameters we evolved within. When a Threaded individual accelerates their neural conduction velocity beyond baseline, they are not casting a speed spell — they are experiencing enhanced myelination driven by proteins encoded in a previously silenced gene cluster. When a Threaded individual generates an electromagnetic pulse, they are not channeling mystical energy — they are activating a biological analog of the electroreceptive systems found in sharks and platypuses, scaled up by gene clusters that encode for novel ion channel proteins.

The Directorate's terminology is clinical: "helix-derived phenotypic expressions." The Unbound call them by simpler names. A Threaded individual who can perceive electrical fields has "reception." One who can accelerate tissue repair has "mending." One who can disrupt another Threaded individual's expression pathways through targeted electromagnetic interference has "severance."

The language you use tells people which side you're on.

### What Is Burning Out?

Threading fails when the primer activates too many clusters simultaneously, when the clusters interact unpredictably, or when the individual's genome cannot sustain the epigenetic rewriting without cascading into error states. The formal term is "expression cascading." The informal term is burning out.

It is not pleasant. The activated clusters produce conflicting protein signals. Tissues attempt to restructure along contradictory templates. Neural pathways overexpress until excitotoxicity kills the neurons themselves. The body, trying to follow too many instructions at once, follows none of them.

The survival rate for uncontrolled expression cascading is approximately four percent.

This is the Directorate's strongest argument: that threading, done wrong, kills you. That the underground clinics lack the precision, the equipment, and the training to thread safely. That regulation exists to prevent people from burning out in basement labs.

The argument is honest. It is also self-serving, because the Directorate could eliminate most burnout deaths overnight by publishing the safe sequencing protocols that AGS has developed. It chooses not to. The burnout rate serves a purpose: it makes threading frightening. Frightened people accept regulation.

## Part V: The Current Crisis

*This section describes the state of the world at the moment the player enters it. It is designed to evolve through admin-driven events.*

### The Helix Census

The Directorate has announced the first mandatory Helix Census — a comprehensive genomic re-registration of every citizen, replacing the birth-registration baseline with a new, higher-resolution scan capable of detecting even trace triplex signatures. The stated purpose is public health monitoring. The actual purpose is the identification and cataloging of every Threaded individual who has avoided detection.

The Census is the single largest political event since the Threshold Act. It creates immediate mechanical consequences:

For Threaded players operating covertly, the Census is an existential threat. High-profile story missions involve forging scan results, sabotaging registration centers, or obtaining Directorate-grade genomic masking technology.

For Baseline players, the Census raises uncomfortable questions. The registration process includes a Phlux assessment — the first time most citizens will learn their own phenotypic flux coefficient. Players who discover they have high Phlux face a choice: report accurately and accept permanent monitoring, underreport and risk criminal liability, or find someone in the Unbound who can help them disappear.

For Directorate-aligned players, the Census is a tool — and a test. Enforcing it means processing thousands of scans, identifying undisclosed Threaded individuals, and deciding what to do with them. The Stabilists want containment. The Ascendants want recruitment. The Indexers want leverage.

### The Primer Shortage

The Unbound's supply chain for PNA primers has been disrupted. Three major synthesis labs in the Strand were raided simultaneously — coordinated action suggesting an intelligence breach within the resistance. Primer quality on the black market has collapsed. Counterfeit primers are circulating, and counterfeit primers cause burnout.

This event creates a resource scarcity that drives player behavior: sequencer characters need to secure new supply lines; combat characters need to protect or raid shipments; crafting characters can attempt to synthesize their own primers (high risk, high reward, high skill requirements). The identity of the informant who enabled the raids is an investigative quest line that branches depending on faction alignment.

### The Basin Bloom

Something is happening in Threshold Basin. The wild-threaded organisms — plants and animals that underwent spontaneous triplex formation from environmental exposure — are exhibiting coordinated behavior. Floral formations are growing in geometric patterns. Animal populations are migrating in synchronized waves. The electromagnetic anomalies that have always characterized the Basin are intensifying and, for the first time, displaying what appear to be structured patterns.

AGS has deployed a research expedition. The Remnant Archive has deployed its own team, independently. Both have gone silent.

This is an event hook for endgame content: the Basin Bloom escalates over time, with admin-driven updates introducing new environmental effects, new wild-threaded creatures, and an unfolding mystery about the nature of the Kowloon clusters themselves. The Remnant Archive's hypothesis — that the clusters were engineered, not evolved — begins to look less like conspiracy and more like warning.

### The Defector

A senior AGS researcher, Dr. Ilya Kazan, has abandoned the Directorate and is attempting to reach the Unbound with a complete copy of the safe sequencing protocols — the clinical data that could eliminate most threading deaths overnight. The Directorate has deployed HIB extraction teams. The Unbound has deployed courier networks. Every faction has a reason to find Kazan first:

The Directorate wants him silenced. The Weavers' Collective wants the protocols. The Null Current wants him as a propaganda asset. The Open Strand Initiative wants him as a public figure. The Indexers — the most dangerous faction here — want to intercept the protocols and use them as leverage to force the licensing system they've been proposing.

Kazan himself has his own agenda, which players will discover is more complicated than any faction assumes.

### The Recurrence Signal

Deep Latitude settlements have been receiving a signal. It is not a broadcast in any conventional sense — it does not travel through electromagnetic spectrum or any detectable medium. It appears directly in the neural tissue of Threaded individuals above a certain Phlux threshold, experienced as a recurring dream, a geometric pattern, a sound that isn't a sound. The pattern is consistent across recipients who have no contact with each other, across thousands of kilometers.

The Directorate is aware of the signal. It has not acknowledged it publicly. Internally, Project Weave — the Ascendant-funded networked consciousness initiative — has pivoted its entire research agenda to understanding the signal's origin.

The Remnant Archive believes the signal is not new. They believe it has been broadcasting for a very long time. They believe the original epigenetic silencing of the Kowloon clusters was not suppression of dangerous capabilities but suppression of the ability to hear this signal. And they believe that as more people are threaded, as more clusters are unlocked, as the collective Phlux of the human species increases — the signal is getting louder.

What it says, and who is sending it, is the central mystery of the Threshold universe. It will not be answered in v0.1. It may not be answered for years. But its presence — a persistent, inexplicable, and increasingly undeniable phenomenon — shapes every faction's calculations and every player's horizon.

## Part VI: The Rules of the World

### Civic Status

Every citizen has a civic status that determines their legal standing, access to services, and interaction with Directorate systems:

**Standard** — Unaugmented Baseline. Full civic rights. Access to housing, employment, medical coverage, transit. This is the default state. It is also a cage, though most people inside it do not feel the bars.

**Conditional** — Modified National. Threaded without Directorate authorization. Civic rights are provisional and can be suspended without appeal. Employment is restricted to licensed categories. Housing is monitored. Medical coverage excludes threading-related conditions. Transit is tracked. Conditional status is visible on every scan, every form, every interaction with institutional systems. It is not imprisonment. It is something more elegant: the constant, ambient awareness that everything you have can be taken.

**Authorized** — Directorate-sanctioned augment. Full civic rights plus access to restricted services, employment in governmental and security roles, and legal protection for expression use. Authorized status is rare and requires either Directorate employment or participation in an AGS research program. It is the carrot that makes the stick possible.

**Suspended** — Active enforcement status. All civic rights revoked. Subject to detention, processing, and correction. Triggered by direct action against the Directorate, repeated violation of threading regulations, or administrative decision. Suspended individuals have no legal standing. They are, in the Directorate's terminology, "in process."

### Faction Alignment

No player chooses a faction at character creation. Faction alignment is earned, shifted, and lost through action. The system tracks two independent variables for each major faction: **Standing** (how much they trust you) and **Suspicion** (how much they watch you). The combination produces nuanced states:

High Standing, Low Suspicion: trusted operative with freedom of movement.
High Standing, High Suspicion: valued but monitored — the Directorate respects your work but doesn't trust your loyalty.
Low Standing, High Suspicion: active target.
Low Standing, Low Suspicion: invisible. Sometimes the most powerful position of all.

Actions for one faction erode standing with the other, but not symmetrically. Small acts of resistance barely register with the Directorate; assassinating a Bureau chief does. Running errands for the Directorate barely registers with the Unbound; turning in a sequencer does.

Players who maintain significant standing with both factions simultaneously enter **Double Agent** states — mechanically rewarded positions with unique missions, abilities, and risks. The double agent gameplay is the most demanding and the most rewarding, and it is designed to feel exactly as precarious as it should.

### Threading as Character Progression

Threading replaces traditional leveling. Players do not gain "experience points" in the conventional sense — they gain **Expression Depth** in specific Kowloon clusters, which unlocks new capabilities along biological skill trees. The rate at which Expression Depth increases is modified by the 5-tier affinity system (class determines cost multipliers) and by the player's Phlux attribute (higher Phlux = faster but noisier progression, with rare unexpected unlocks).

Death carries meaningful consequences scaled to civic status. Standard citizens respawn at medical facilities with minor resource loss. Conditional citizens respawn at underground clinics with significant resource loss and a temporary increase in Directorate Suspicion. Suspended citizens respawn at resistance safehouses with major resource loss and a risk of permanent Expression degradation — the in-universe explanation being that uncontrolled resuscitation in field conditions can damage triplex integrity.

## Part VII: The Questions That Drive the World

Threshold is not a game about good versus evil. It is a game about five questions that do not have clean answers:

**Does safety justify control?** The Directorate's case is genuine. Unregulated threading kills people. The underground clinics are dangerous. The technology is powerful enough to destabilize civilization. Is the Directorate's monopoly a necessary evil — or just evil?

**Does freedom justify risk?** The Unbound's case is also genuine. People have a right to their own genome. The Directorate hoards technology that could alleviate suffering. But the Unbound's methods — including threading people who might die from it — carry costs that its rhetoric rarely acknowledges.

**What are we becoming?** Threading changes what humans can do. Does it change what humans are? The Threaded are still human — but they are human in a way that Baselines are not. The social, political, and philosophical implications of a species that is diverging within itself are the deep current beneath every faction conflict.

**Who silenced the Kowloon clusters?** The Remnant Archive's question. If the capabilities encoded in those gene clusters were deliberately suppressed — not by evolution, not by developmental programming, but by an external agent — then the entire framework of the Threshold crisis changes. Threading is not innovation. It is restoration. And the thing that silenced humanity once may have reasons to do it again.

**What is the signal?** The question beneath all the other questions. The one that no faction can answer, that no amount of political maneuvering or military force can resolve. Something is communicating with the Threaded, and it has been waiting for them to be able to hear it.

These questions are not for the game to answer. They are for the players to argue about, to take sides on, to build their identities around. The admin team's role is to keep the questions alive — to introduce events that complicate every faction's position, that prevent any side from being obviously right, and that remind players that the world of Threshold is not a puzzle to be solved but a condition to be navigated.

## Epilogue: Sera

The patient's integration cycle completed at 04:17. Sera watched the readout stabilize — third strand propagation at 94.2%, expression pathway formation nominal, Phlux coefficient settling at 0.73. High, but not dangerously so.

He would sleep for ten hours. When he woke, his helix signature would be detectable by any Directorate scanner within three meters. His civic status would drop to Conditional the moment he passed through a checkpoint. His employment, his housing, his medical coverage — all of it would become provisional. He would be, in the Directorate's language, a Modified National. In the Unbound's language, one of them.

In his own language, he would be something he hadn't been when he walked in: capable.

Sera packed the sequencer. Cleaned the surfaces. Checked the exit corridor for signal activity. The atmospheric scanners completed their sweep every forty minutes; she had a window of six before the next one. Time to move.

She did not think about the signal. She had been dreaming the pattern for three weeks now — the same geometric structure, the same frequency that wasn't a frequency, the same sense of something vast and patient and very, very old turning its attention toward a species that was just learning to hear.

She did not think about it because thinking about it didn't help. What helped was work. Thread the next patient. Train the next sequencer. Keep the clinics running. Keep people alive long enough for the questions to matter.

The door closed behind her. The corridor was empty. Somewhere above, in a sky she couldn't see, the Directorate's atmospheric emitters painted their proprietary blue across the upper atmosphere, and the scanning relays completed their cataloging of fourteen thousand biometric signatures, and the signal continued, as it had continued for longer than anyone knew, waiting with the patience of something that had nowhere else to be.

Sera descended into the dark. There was work to do.
